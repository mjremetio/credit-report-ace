give me a list of ALL fcra violations that we can find on a credit report, are actionable and we can sue on

Goatmez Expert said:
As a matter of law, not every error on a credit report is automatically actionable. Under the FCRA, you generally need:

Inaccurate or incomplete reporting

A violation of a specific statutory duty

Actual damages (or willfulness for statutory damages)

Proper dispute (when required under §1681s-2(b))

Below is a comprehensive list of actionable FCRA violations that can appear on a credit report and form the basis of a lawsuit, grouped by statute section.

I. ACTIONABLE VIOLATIONS BY CONSUMER REPORTING AGENCIES (CRAs)
1. §1681e(b) – Failure to Follow Reasonable Procedures to Assure Maximum Possible Accuracy
You can sue if a CRA reports:

Accounts that do not belong to you (mixed file)

Incorrect balances

Incorrect payment history

Incorrect dates (open date, last payment date, delinquency date)

Incorrect account status (charged off, late, etc.)

Incorrect personal identifiers causing file mixing

Reporting a debt twice (duplicate tradelines)

Reporting a discharged bankruptcy debt as active

Reporting settled debt as unpaid

Reporting paid collection as unpaid

Reporting incorrect high balance

Reporting incorrect credit limit (when omission materially misleads)

Key: Must show the report was inaccurate AND procedures were unreasonable.

2. §1681i – Failure to Conduct Reasonable Reinvestigation After Dispute
After you dispute with a CRA:

They must:

Conduct reasonable investigation

Forward all relevant information to furnisher

Complete within 30 days

Delete or correct inaccurate information

Notify you of results

You can sue if they:

Fail to investigate

Conduct superficial “automated” investigation only

Fail to forward documentation you provided

Fail to delete unverifiable information

Reinsert deleted information without notice

Fail to send reinvestigation results

Ignore identity theft documentation

Continue reporting after investigation proves inaccuracy

3. §1681c – Obsolete Information
Reporting beyond allowed time limits:

Collections older than 7 years

Charge-offs older than 7 years

Late payments older than 7 years

Civil judgments older than 7 years (now largely removed)

Paid tax liens older than 7 years

Bankruptcies older than 10 years

Accounts re-aged to extend reporting time

Re-aging is highly actionable.

4. §1681b – Impermissible Purpose
A CRA may only furnish your report for permissible purposes.

Violations include:

Hard inquiry without permissible purpose

Pulling report without consent

Employment report without authorization

Report accessed for non-credit reasons

Report accessed by debt collector without valid debt

Each impermissible pull is a separate violation.

5. §1681g – Failure to Provide File Disclosure
If a CRA:

Fails to provide full file disclosure

Omits tradelines in disclosure

Refuses free annual report

Fails to provide source of information

Fails to disclose entities that pulled your report

6. §1681k – Failure to Maintain Strict Procedures for Public Record Information (Employment Context)
If CRA reports public record info for employment purposes without:

Notifying you at time of report OR

Maintaining strict procedures to ensure complete and up-to-date info

II. ACTIONABLE VIOLATIONS BY FURNISHERS (§1681s-2(b))
You cannot sue under §1681s-2(a) directly.
You CAN sue under §1681s-2(b) AFTER a CRA dispute.

After CRA notifies furnisher, they must:

Conduct reasonable investigation

Review all information from CRA

Report results to CRA

Correct inaccurate information

Report corrections to all CRAs

Delete unverifiable information

You can sue if furnisher:

Verifies inaccurate account

Fails to review documentation

Fails to investigate identity theft

Fails to correct balance

Fails to correct payment history

Fails to correct account status

Fails to correct date of first delinquency

Continues reporting after proving inaccuracy

Reports debt discharged in bankruptcy

Reports settled debt as unpaid

Reports incorrect ownership of debt

Fails to report dispute notation after CRA dispute

Fails to correct after settlement agreement

III. DIRECT DISPUTE VIOLATIONS (§1681s-2(a)(8))
If you dispute directly with furnisher (per regulations):

They must:

Conduct reasonable investigation

Complete within 30 days

Notify you of results

Violation if:

They ignore direct dispute

They label dispute frivolous improperly

They fail to investigate

They fail to correct

IV. IDENTITY THEFT VIOLATIONS
§1681c-2 – Blocking Identity Theft Information
If you submit:

Identity theft affidavit

Police report

CRA must block within 4 business days.

Actionable if:

They refuse to block

They delay blocking

They reinsert without proper certification

§1681s-2(a)(6) – Reporting Identity Theft Debt After Notice
Furnisher may not continue reporting identity theft debt after proper notice.

V. FAILURE TO MARK ACCOUNT AS DISPUTED
Under §1681s-2(a)(3) & §1681s-2(b)

After receiving dispute:

Furnisher must:

Report account as “disputed”

Violation if:

They fail to mark disputed during investigation

They remove dispute notation prematurely

Courts widely recognize this as actionable under §1681s-2(b).

VI. REINSERTION VIOLATIONS (§1681i(a)(5)(B))
If CRA deletes account after dispute, they cannot reinsert unless:

Furnisher certifies accuracy

CRA notifies consumer within 5 business days

Violation if:

No certification

No notice

No new investigation

VII. MIXED FILE / MERGED FILE
Highly actionable:

Another consumer’s accounts reporting on your file

Social security mix

Name mix

Family member accounts mixed

Similar name confusion

Usually §1681e(b) + §1681i claim.

VIII. FALSE DATE OF FIRST DELINQUENCY (DOFD)
Under §1681s-2(a)(5)

Furnisher must accurately report DOFD.

Violation if:

Re-aging account

Manipulating delinquency date

Resetting 7-year clock

Very strong litigation claim.

IX. FAILURE TO CORRECT AFTER SETTLEMENT OR PAYMENT
If:

Settlement agreement states “delete”

Paid collection still reporting balance

Paid charge-off reporting balance

Account reporting past due after paid

Potential §1681s-2(b) violation after dispute.

X. BANKRUPTCY MISREPORTING
Actionable issues:

Reporting discharged debt with balance

Reporting included in bankruptcy as active collection

Reporting incorrect chapter

Reporting incorrect discharge date

Reporting reaffirmed debt inaccurately

XI. EMPLOYMENT BACKGROUND REPORT VIOLATIONS
Failure to provide copy before adverse action

Failure to provide summary of rights

Failure to provide adverse action notice

(Technically FCRA employment provisions)

XII. DAMAGES & STANDARDS
To sue successfully:

Negligent violation → §1681o

Actual damages

Attorney fees

Willful violation → §1681n

$100–$1,000 statutory damages

Punitive damages

Attorney fees

Willfulness = knowing or reckless disregard.

Correct collections

Emotional distress without violation

There must be:

Failure after dispute (for furnishers)

You said:
what about dunning letters?

Goatmez Expert said:
Excellent question.

Dunning letters are primarily FDCPA issues — not FCRA issues — but they can overlap.

Let’s break this down precisely.

1️⃣ What Is a Dunning Letter?
A dunning letter is the initial written communication from a debt collector attempting to collect a debt.

Under FDCPA §1692g, the collector must provide a validation notice within 5 days of initial communication.

It must include:

Amount of the debt

Name of creditor

Statement that consumer has 30 days to dispute

Statement that debt will be assumed valid if not disputed

Statement that collector will obtain verification if disputed

Statement that collector will provide name of original creditor upon request

This is strictly FDCPA territory.

2️⃣ Is a Dunning Letter an FCRA Violation?
Not by itself.

However, it can become an FCRA issue in specific situations.

3️⃣ Where Dunning Letters Intersect with FCRA
A. Failure to Mark Account as Disputed (Major Overlap)
If:

You dispute the debt within 30 days under FDCPA

The collector reports to CRA

They fail to mark the account as disputed

That becomes an FCRA violation under:

§1681s-2(a)(3) (duty to report dispute)

§1681s-2(b) after CRA dispute

This is very actionable.

B. Reporting Before Sending Validation Notice
Under FDCPA:

If a collector reports a debt to a CRA before sending a proper validation notice, courts have found this may violate:

§1692e (false/misleading representation)

§1692g (overshadowing rights)

But this is FDCPA, not FCRA.

C. Reporting During the 30-Day Validation Period
If:

You timely dispute under FDCPA

Collector continues collection activity

Collector reports without noting dispute

That can create:

FDCPA violation

FCRA violation (if not marked disputed)

D. Reporting Without Proper Authority
If:

Collector has no ownership

Cannot validate debt

Reports anyway

After CRA dispute, this becomes an FCRA §1681s-2(b) failure to reasonably investigate.

E. Reporting Time-Barred Debt
If a dunning letter attempts to collect time-barred debt and:

Fails to disclose statute expiration (some jurisdictions require)

Reports it in misleading way

That’s typically FDCPA.

But if reporting is inaccurate (wrong date of first delinquency), that becomes FCRA.

4️⃣ Important Distinction
Issue	FDCPA	FCRA
Improper validation notice	✅	❌
Overshadowing language	✅	❌
Threats / misleading language	✅	❌
Reporting without dispute notation	❌	✅
Failure to investigate after CRA dispute	❌	✅
Re-aging	❌	✅
Inaccurate balance reporting	❌	✅
5️⃣ Can a Dunning Letter Itself Appear on a Credit Report?
No.

The letter itself does not appear.

What appears is the collection tradeline.

So the violation must tie back to:

Inaccurate reporting

Failure to mark disputed

Failure to investigate

Re-aging

Incorrect balance

6️⃣ Strategic Reality
Most consumers confuse:

FDCPA violations (letter problems)

FCRA violations (reporting problems)

However, the strongest cases often combine both:

Example:

Collector sends defective dunning letter (FDCPA)

Reports inaccurate debt (FCRA)

Fails to mark disputed (FCRA)

Verifies without investigation (FCRA)

That becomes leverage.

3️⃣ How Courts Treat Validation Disputes vs Credit Reporting Disputes
This is critical.

A. FDCPA Validation Dispute (Direct to Collector)
When you dispute within 30 days:

Collector must:

Cease collection

Obtain verification

Mail verification

This does NOT automatically trigger FCRA duties.

Courts treat this as separate.

You cannot sue under FCRA just because they failed to validate.

B. CRA Dispute (Triggers §1681s-2(b))
When you dispute through Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion:

The CRA notifies the furnisher.

Now the furnisher must:

Conduct reasonable investigation

Review all relevant information

Correct inaccuracies

Report results to all CRAs

This is where FCRA liability attaches.

Important Court Distinction
Courts repeatedly say:

A furnisher’s duty under FCRA arises only after receiving notice from a CRA — not directly from the consumer.

So:

Direct dispute alone → weak FCRA claim
CRA dispute → actionable FCRA claim

How Courts Evaluate “Reasonable Investigation”
Courts look at:

Did they review underlying documents?

Did they just confirm internal data?

Did they verify automatically?

Did they ignore supporting evidence?

Did they fail to examine identity theft documents?

Automated e-OSCAR verification alone is often attacked as insufficient in complex disputes.

Practical Litigation Formula

Defective dunning letter (FDCPA)

Reporting inaccurate tradeline

Collector verifies without real investigation

Fails to mark disputed

Continues reporting

That creates dual statutory exposure.
